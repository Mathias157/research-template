# Academic Writing Principles

30 prose-quality principles for writing compelling research papers, organized into 6 categories. These principles guide the paper-read skill and all writing-adjacent workflows.

## Clarity: Say Exactly What You Mean

**Clarity 1: Start with the contribution.** Lead paragraphs with what this paper *does*, not background. Don't make readers wade through literature before learning why they should care.

**Clarity 2: One sentence per main idea.** Long, winding sentences hide your meaning. If you need 30 words to express one idea, you probably have two ideas. Split them.

**Clarity 3: Use active voice (usually).** "We optimized the model" beats "The model was optimized." It's clearer who did what. Exception: when the agent truly doesn't matter, passive is fine.

**Clarity 4: Replace jargon with precision.** "Sophisticated approach" means nothing. "Reinforcement learning with attention mechanisms" means something. Jargon is fine only if your audience knows it; otherwise, define or replace.

**Clarity 5: Define terms once, then use them consistently.** Don't call it "the algorithm", then "the approach", then "the method". Pick a name and stick with it.

**Clarity 6: Use concrete examples.** Abstract descriptions are skimmed; concrete examples are read. When explaining a concept, show an example immediately after.

## Structure: Build a Skeleton Your Reader Can Follow

**Structure 1: Reverse-pyramid introduction.** Lead with the most important claim, then work outward to context. Readers should know the paper's contribution by the end of the first paragraph.

**Structure 2: Topic sentences first.** Each paragraph should start with its main claim, then support it. A reader should be able to skim topic sentences and understand the paper.

**Structure 3: Group related ideas in paragraphs.** One paragraph = one idea. If a paragraph covers two different points, split it.

**Structure 4: Use headings liberally.** Headings break up visual monotony and let readers navigate quickly. A paper without headings is harder to scan than one with them.

**Structure 5: Build transitions between sections.** Don't jump from one idea to the next. Use a sentence or two to show *why* the next section follows logically.

**Structure 6: Put key results in the main text.** If a result is important enough to include, it's important enough to discuss in the main narrative. Banishing key findings to appendices makes them invisible.

## Evidence: Back Up Every Claim

**Evidence 1: Distinguish observation from interpretation.** Say "We observed X" or "The data show X"; don't say "This suggests Y" when you mean "We found Y". If you infer something, label the inference explicitly.

**Evidence 2: Quote sparingly; paraphrase often.** Quotes are for when the exact wording matters. Most of the time, paraphrase the idea in your own words (and cite the source). Your writing will be more cohesive.

**Evidence 3: Cite the original source, not a secondary cite.** If Smith cites Jones' finding, cite Jones, not Smith. Cite chains are unreliable; originals are authoritative.

**Evidence 4: Acknowledge limitations, don't hide them.** A limitation you name is a limitation you've thought about. Readers trust authors who are honest about what they didn't do.

**Evidence 5: Use numbers over adjectives.** "Significantly faster" means nothing without context. "3.2× faster on standard benchmarks" means something. If you have numbers, use them.

**Evidence 6: Show methodology before claiming results.** A reader can't evaluate "our approach outperforms X" without understanding what your approach is. Describe method first, then results.

## Precision: Make Your Arguments Airtight

**Precision 1: Avoid weasel words.** "Somewhat", "in many cases", "arguably", "tends to" — these weaken claims and signal uncertainty you probably don't feel. Replace with concrete qualifications ("in 7 of 10 test cases") or stronger claims.

**Precision 2: Define your scope explicitly.** Say "this approach works for image classification" not "this approach works for machine learning". Scope limits claims and makes them credible.

**Precision 3: Separate necessary from sufficient conditions.** X is necessary for Y means you can't have Y without X. X is sufficient for Y means X alone guarantees Y. Don't confuse them.

**Precision 4: Use "if and only if" sparingly, and precisely.** This phrasing is strong and should match strong logical relationships. Misuse signals sloppy thinking.

**Precision 5: Quantify uncertainty.** Instead of "the results may vary", say "95% confidence interval: X–Y" or "we tested on 3 datasets; 2 showed the effect". Numbers build trust.

**Precision 6: State your assumptions up front.** Readers can evaluate your work only if they know what you assumed. Don't bury assumptions in method sections.

## Audience: Write for People Who Will Skim

**Audience 1: Assume readers are busy.** They will skim. Design your paper so that skimming gives them the main ideas. Put key results in figure captions, conclusions at the end of sections.

**Audience 2: Anticipate questions.** What would a skeptical reader ask? Address those questions before they arise. "Why not compare to X?" deserves an answer; put it in the related work.

**Audience 3: Use visuals strategically.** A figure should communicate one idea quickly. If your figure is cluttered or ambiguous, it fails. Caption it completely so readers understand it without reading the text.

**Audience 4: Write the abstract last.** Write it after the paper is done. An early abstract will drift as your writing evolves; a final abstract is accurate.

**Audience 5: Revise for the reader, not the writer.** When you revise, ask "Does this sentence make sense to someone who isn't me?" If the answer is no, rewrite it.

**Audience 6: Use second-person sparingly; never assume the reader's knowledge.** You've spent months on this work; your reader hasn't. Avoid "as you know" or "obviously". Explain.

## Revision: Polish Until It Shines

**Revision 1: First draft is always messy.** Don't expect to write prose-quality work in one pass. Plan to revise thoroughly.

**Revision 2: Read aloud to catch awkwardness.** Your ear catches rhythm problems your eyes miss. If a sentence is hard to read aloud, it's hard to read.

**Revision 3: Cut 10% on a second pass.** Academic writing tends toward padding. After your first draft, go through and delete one sentence per paragraph that doesn't advance the argument. Your paper will be tighter.

**Revision 4: Check citations for accuracy.** Before submission, verify that every citation is correct and that you've cited the right version (e.g., conference version, not tech report). Cite errors damage credibility.

**Revision 5: Proofread multiple times.** Read for grammar once. Read for clarity once. Read for logic once. Mixing these passes causes you to miss things.

**Revision 6: Get feedback from someone outside your field.** An outsider will tell you where you're unclear or making unjustified leaps. Insiders are too familiar with your thinking; they'll miss gaps.

---

## Special Rule: Fidelity (No Extrapolation)

**Fidelity 1: Every claim must be traceable to a source.** When writing about others' work or previous findings, cite the source. If you can't point to where it came from, don't write it.

**Fidelity 2: Never extrapolate beyond what the data show.** If your results show X, you can discuss what X implies about Y (with caveats). Don't draw conclusions your results don't support.

**Fidelity 3: Quote sparingly and accurately.** When you do quote, verify the quote is exact. Paraphrasing with citation is usually better than quoting.

---

## Using These Principles

These principles support:

- **Paper reading and assessment** (paper-read skill): clarity, structure, evidence guides critical reading
- **Writing and revision** (any writing task): refer to relevant principles during drafting and revision
- **Wiki documentation** (fidelity discipline): ensure every wiki page follows clarity, structure, evidence, and fidelity principles

When writing or revising, pick 2–3 principles to focus on in a single pass. Don't try to apply all 30 at once.
