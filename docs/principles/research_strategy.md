# Research Strategy Principles

Carlini-inspired framework for research project evaluation and strategic decision-making. These principles guide the research companion and research strategist workflows.

## RS1: Novelty Test

**Every research direction should pass a novelty check.** Ask: "Is this genuinely novel, or am I optimizing a problem someone else solved?" A novel contribution doesn't have to be revolutionary—it can be a clever angle, a cross-field bridge, or a practical optimization—but it must be *something others haven't done*.

Apply this when evaluating new ideas or pivoting existing work.

## RS2: Comparative Advantage Mapping

**Identify your unique corner of the research space.** You have unfair advantages: technical skills, domain knowledge, cross-field bridges, access to data/compute/collaborators, or a distinctive perspective. Your best research lives at the intersection of these strengths and important unsolved problems.

Map your advantages across technical skills, domain expertise, cross-field bridges, access, and unique perspective.

## RS3: Conclusion-First Test

**If you can't write a compelling conclusion before doing the work, the idea isn't ready.** Draft an abstract and conclusion for the paper you would write. Does it feel like a paper you'd be excited to read? Does the conclusion matter?

This test applies at project start and again when pivoting.

## RS4: Riskiest Assumption

**Every project rests on assumptions. Identify the riskiest one—the assumption that, if false, would kill the work.** Prioritize testing this assumption before sinking months into execution.

Use this when designing experiments or planning next steps.

## RS5: Scooping Risk Assessment

**Evaluate whether others are converging on the same idea.** Check recent preprints, track active groups, and assess your execution speed relative to likely competitors. If scooping is imminent, either accelerate, find a differentiated angle, or deliberately pursue simultaneous independent work (which can still have value by validating importance).

Mitigation strategies: pursue unique novelty angles (RS1), execute faster by narrowing scope, or find a cross-field bridge others haven't explored (RS2).

## RS6: Triage Framework (Continue / Pivot / Kill)

**When deciding whether to continue a project, evaluate it holistically across five dimensions:**

- **Results**: Core hypothesis validated (Continue) vs. interesting side results (Pivot) vs. core assumption disproven (Kill)
- **Competition**: Still ahead or unique (Continue) vs. others approaching differently (Pivot) vs. scooped (Kill)
- **Impact**: Conclusion still compelling (Continue) vs. original framing weak but results support different framing (Pivot) vs. can't write compelling conclusion (Kill)
- **Effort remaining**: Manageable (Continue) vs. moderate with different goal (Pivot) vs. large with uncertain success (Kill)
- **Motivation**: Engaged (Continue) vs. curious about pivot (Pivot) vs. dreading it (Kill)

When recommending a pivot, be specific: what is the new framing and how does it preserve your work?

## RS7: Cross-Field Bridges

**The richest source of comparative advantage is unusual combinations of fields.** If you span energy systems + machine learning + policy, you can see opportunities neither energy systems specialists nor ML specialists can see. Actively cultivate cross-field thinking.

Use this when brainstorming new directions or assessing your unique positioning.

## RS8: Impact = Skill × Domain Importance

**Your research impact depends on two factors: your skill in the domain and the importance of the domain at this moment.** You may be highly skilled in a field that's declining in importance, or working on an increasingly crucial problem where you're still ramping up. Assess both dimensions explicitly.

When evaluating research directions, estimate domain importance (via recent funding, conference trends, practical deployment, regulatory pressure, saturation) and your skill level, then multiply.

---

## Using These Principles

These principles support:

- **Idea evaluation** (research-companion skill): brainstorm → diverge → evaluate using these principles
- **Strategic planning** (research-strategist agent): assess projects, map advantages, forecast impact
- **Research triage** (ongoing): should I continue, pivot, or kill this project?

Reference the principle by its number (RS1–RS8) when discussing research decisions.
